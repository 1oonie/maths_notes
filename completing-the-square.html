Completing the square

Last updated: 14 Oct 2024

1. How do I complete the square?

Presented with the monic quadratic x 2 + 2 x − 2 , a normal person will not be able to divine the roots at a glance.

The uninitiated mathematician might even feel the need to pull out a calculator and tap in the “quadratic formula”.

Luckily, we have seen the light and will realise that we can rewrite the equation in the form ( x + p ) 2 + q . It is easy to see that p must be 1 as ( x + 1 ) 2 = x 2 + 2 x + 1 . We must now subtract p 2 so our constant term is 0 and then tack on our original constant term to find q. Now that we have rewritten our quadratic, it is time to find our roots.

For non-monic quadratics, we just factorise the x 2 and x terms by the x 2 coefficient to get a monic quadratic.

For some non-monics we can divide the whole quadratic by the x 2 coefficient.

2. Why should I complete the square?

Completing the square is one of the greatest tasks one can do in mathematics. Nothing quite beats the feeling when right hand side fraction turns out to be a square number over another square number. Surdless roots are quite something... Just as the poet seeks refuge in the Great Poems (To Autumn etcetera), so too the mathematical connoisseur takes refuge in orthogonal quadrilaterals.

The Author asserts that completing the square is the purest and most explicit way to discern the roots of a second-degree polynomial.

It is important to not unsee the light and succumb to the temptation presented by the “quadratic” “formula”. Such temptations can be resisted by reciting this (snappy) mantra: “the general equation to solve second-degree polynomials is derived from completing the square on a x 2 + b x + c ”. The Author finds that the most effective number of repititions is at least 10.

3. When should I complete the square?

As with most things in life, with great power comes great responsibility. Complete the square only when the roots are not able to be found by staring at the constant term and x coefficient for approximately 10 seconds. For example, given the quadratic x 2 + 7 x + 12 it should be immediately obvious that it factorises into ( x + 3 ) ( x + 4 ) . Clearly, the power of completing the square would not be necessary here.

With all other quadratics (unmonicable non-monics especially), the Author strenuously recommends indiscriminate use. Not only will your fractional multiplication and division skills thank you, but you will be assured that you have achieved the correct roots.
